\section{Probability and Statistics}

\subsection{Introduction (Optional section)}

We are preparing a tea party for $N$ guests.
Let us denote the average number of tea cups each participant would like to drink by $\mu_{tea}$.
In statistics, this average is called the \textit{population mean}, meaning simply that it refers to an average over the whole group which is under consideration -
 all the $N$ guests in our case.
 \vspace{3mm}

The total number of tea cups we should prepare is $T=N \times\mu_{tea}$. We do not know $\mu_{tea}$ and
it would take too long to ask each of the guests how many tea cups they would like to have.
So instead, we \textit{sample independently} people in the guest list: we randomly choose one, call and ask how many tea cups they would like to drink and write down the answer as $x_1$. We then randomly call another one (could accidentally be the same one) and write the second answer as $x_2$. We continue $m$ times.
To estimate the population mean, $\mu_{tea}$, we use  the average of the answers which in statistics is called the \textit{sample mean} and is given by
$$\hat{\mu}_{tea} \equiv \frac{1}{m}\sum_{k=1}^{m}x_k.$$
Finally, we \textit{predict} that we will need $T= N \times \hat{\mu}_{tea}$ cups.

The little "hat" symbol, $\hat{}$ , is there to point out that the right hand side of the above definition is what we \textit{choose} as an \textit{estimator} for the population mean $\mu_{tea}$.  The linearity of the expectation value ensures that the expectation value of the estimator $\hat{\mu}_{tea}$ is indeed equal to   $\mu_{tea}$. An estimator whose expectation value is equal to the quantity it is designed to estimate, is called an \textit{unbiased} estimator.
$\hat{\mu}_{tea}$ may seem an obvious choice since it has a form similar to that of $\mu_{tea}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{k=1}^{N}x_k$.
However, if we tried to estimate the \textit{variance}, $V(x)$, of the personal tea consumption among the guests using the same logic, that is, by choosing $\frac{1}{m}\sum_{k=1}^{m}(x_k-\hat{\mu}_{tea})^2$ as an estimator, we would find, perhaps surprisingly, that we are consistently off target:
the expectation value of $\frac{1}{m}\sum_{k=1}^{m}(x_k-\hat{\mu}_{tea})^2$ is \textit{different} than the variance $V(x)$ and therefore $\frac{1}{m}\sum_{k=1}^{m}(x_k-\hat{\mu}_{tea})^2$ is a \textit{biased} estimator.
We will return to this point in the next sections.
\vspace{3mm}

The above way in which we predicted $T$ can be formulated as the following \textit{learning algorithm}:
\begin{itemize}
	\item Enter an integer $m<N$
	\item Set $k=1$ and $\hat{\mu}_{tea}=0$
	\item For $k\le m$, set $x_k = \text{answer from guest number \textit{k}}$
	\item Set $\hat{\mu}_{tea}=\frac{x_k+(k-1)\hat{\mu}_{tea}}{k}$ and $k=k+1$
	\item Once $k=m$, return $T=N\times \hat{\mu}_{tea}$
\end{itemize}

We call this algorithm a learning algorithm because we learn from our experience: each time we ask another person $\hat{\mu}_{tea}$ changes - \textit{probably} getting closer to $\mu_{tea}$ - and therefore also our prediction of $T$ improve. Note that we might get unlucky and ask a non-representative set of $m$ people that particularly drink much more or much less than the average guest, so  $\hat{\mu}_{tea}$ might be a bad approximation of $\mu_{tea}$.
To not disappoint many of the guests on one hand and to not prepare too much tea cups on the other hand, we can require that our algorithm will keep running until, for example, it becomes \textit{probable} that $\hat{\mu}_{tea}$ is accurate enough. For example, we can choose an accuracy parameter $\epsilon>0$ and require that $|\hat{\mu}_{tea}-\mu_{tea}|<\epsilon$.
The question is how many people we should ask to ensure this requirement holds?

If we knew  the \textit{probability distribution} (or more precisely, since we are dealing with discrete numbers here, the \textit{probability mass function}) of tea cup consumption among our guests, we could calculate the answer to that question. However, even without knowing the exact \textit{probability distribution} we can still adapt  very general assumptions about its properties, such as its variance, in order to get an upper bounds for the probability that our error will be bigger than $\epsilon$.

\vspace{3mm}
The above algorithm is a learning algorithms that can obviously be implemented by a traditional computer program.
In many cases, the amount of data generated by the modern abundance of computerized sensors and memory is vast as well as too multi-dimensional, involving \textit{random vectors} with thousands of components and not only a single random variable such as the number of tea cups. Such data must be processed by specially designed algorithms, called machine learning algorithms, but the main principles demonstrated in above still remain: Choose estimators (that can be very complicated and may depend on many variables) in order to make predictions and take enough samples to be sure that, with high probability, the errors are within a required range. As seen from the above example,  probabilistic and statisticlal concepts take important part in such algorithm design and therefore we will now make a short review of those.


\subsection{Probability Space}
The basic object in probability theory is that of a \emph{probability space} which consists of two ingredients: the \textit{sample space} and the \textit{probability function}.
\begin{definition}
A \emph{sample space} $\Omega$ is a set that contains all possible outcomes. $\omega\in\Omega$ denotes a single outcome.
\end{definition}
Examples:
\begin{itemize}
\item Coin toss: $\Omega=\left\{ H,T\right\} $
\item Three coin tosses $\Omega=\left\{ H,T\right\}^3 $
\item Toss a coin until heads comes up: $\Omega=\left\{ T^{n-1}H:n\in\N\right\} $
\item The number of times each of the 1000 product web-pages in an online store is viewed by an online customer
 $\Omega=\{ n_1, n_2,....,n_{1000}\} $ with $n_i \in \N$
\item The number of times each of the 1000 product web-pages in an online store is viewed by an online customer
and the number of each product that eventually is bought: $\Omega=\{ n_1, n_2,....,n_{1000}\} \times \{ N_1, N_2,...N_{1000} \}$ with $n_i, N_i \in \N$
\item Height, weight, blood pressure, sex of patients with a medical condition and the success or failure of an experimental drug  : $\Omega=[0,\infty)\times [0,\infty) \times[0,\infty) \times \{M,F\}\times \{Yes,No\} $
\end{itemize}
\vspace{3mm}

Suppose someone tossed a coin three times and told us that the result contained more heads than tails. This information does not define a unique element in $\Omega$, but it tells us that the outcome is an element in the subset $\left\{ (H,H,H),(H,H,T),(H,T,H),(T,H,H)\right\} $.
\begin{definition}
An \emph{event} $A$ is any subset of possible outcomes, $A\subseteq\Omega$.
\end{definition}
Quick reminder:
\begin{itemize}
\item $A^{c}$ (or sometimes $\overline{A}$) denotes the \textit{complement} of $A$, $A^{c}=\Omega\backslash A$ . In other words, $A^{c}$ is the event "$A$ did not occur".
\item We say that $A$ and $B$ are \textit{disjoint events} if $A\cap B=\emptyset$.
\item $\Omega$ and $\emptyset$ are also events. If $A$ and $B$ are two events, then $A\cup B$ is also an event, and so is $A\cap B$.
\end{itemize}

Intuitively, a probability function assigns to an event a number which quantifies our knowledge or belief about the possibility of observing any outcome in that event.


\begin{definition}
	A \textit{probability space} is a tuple \footnote{For our needs this simple and intuitive definition is sufficient, though, richer definitions exist.} $(\Omega, \Dc)$ where $\Omega$ is a \emph{sample space} and $\Dc:2^{\Omega}\rightarrow\R$ is a probability function such that
	\begin{enumerate}
		\item $\Dc(\Omega)=1$
		\item for all $\omega\in\Omega, \Dc(\omega)\in[0,1]$
		\item for all $A,B\subseteq\Omega$ such that $A\cap B=\emptyset,$ we have $\Dc(A \cup B)=\Dc(A)+\Dc(B).$
	\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\begin{example}
	Suppose we throw two fair dice, then $\Omega=\left\{ 1,\dots,6\right\} ^{2}$ and $\Dc\left(\left(i,j\right)\right)=\frac{1}{36}$
\end{example}

\begin{exercise}
	For all $A,B\subseteq\Omega$: $\Dc(A\cup B)=\Dc(A)+\Dc(B)-\Dc(A\cap B)$
\end{exercise}
Solution:\\
$A\cup B=(A\setminus B)\cup(B\setminus A)\cup(A\cap B)\; ,A=(A\setminus B)\cup(A\cap B)$ and $B=(B\setminus A)\cup(A\cap B).$
$$\Dc(A\cup B)=\Dc(A\setminus B)+\Dc(B\setminus A)+\Dc(A\cap B)=\Dc(A)-\Dc(A\cap B)+\Dc(B) -  \cancel{\Dc(A\cap B)}  + \cancel{\Dc(A\cap B)} $$

\begin{definition}
	$A,B\subseteq\Omega$ are called \emph{independent} if the occurrence of one does not affect the probability of occurrence of the other. Consequently: $$\Dc(A\cap B) =\Dc(A)\cdot\Dc(B).$$
\end{definition}

\begin{exercise}
	Show that if $A$ and $B$ are independent then $A$ and $B^c$ are also independent.
\end{exercise}
Solution: $\Dc(A)=\Dc(A\cap B)+\Dc(A\cap B^c)\Rightarrow\Dc(A\cap B^c)=\Dc(A)(1-\Dc(B))=\Dc(A)\cdot\Dc(B^c).$\\


\begin{theorem}[The union bound] Let 	$(\Omega, \Dc)$ be a probability space. The probability function is \emph{sub-additive}, i.e., for any sequence $(A_k)$ of events,
	\[
	\Dc(\cup_{k=1}^\infty A_k) \le \sum_{k=1} ^\infty \Dc(A_k)
	\]
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
	Let $B_1=A_1$. For each $k \in \{2,3,\ldots\}$, let $B_k = A_k
	\setminus \cup_{i=1}^{k-1} A_i$. Note that the $B_1...B_k$ are disjoint, and
	$\cup_{i=1}^k A_i=\cup_{i=1}^k B_i$.
	Also, since $B_k\subseteq A_k$,
	$\Dc(B_k) \le \Dc(A_k)$
	for every $k \in \N$. It follows that
	\[
	\Dc(\cup_{k=1}^\infty A_k) = \Dc(\cup_{k=1}^\infty B_k) = \sum_{k=1}
	^\infty \Dc(B_k) \le \sum_{k=1}
	^\infty \Dc(A_k)~.
	\]
\end{proof}


\subsection{Random Variables, discrete and continuous}

So far we have asked questions like: does an event happen or not and what is the probability of the event. But what about different questions, such as, "how much." For example, if we get a dollar if a coin flip turns head, we can ask how many dollars would we get after 3 rounds?  In order to answer this question, we should identify the sample space, which is
$\Omega=\{HHH,HHT,HTH,THH,HTT,THT,TTH,TTT\}$. Then we would need to define a reward function that will quantify ones's profit for each outcome of the experiment ($\omega\in \Omega$):
$X(HHH)=3,X(HHT)=2,$ and so on. This quantifying function $X$ is called a \emph{random variable} (RV).
\begin{definition}
	Given a probability space $\left(\Omega,\Dc\right)$, a real-valued \emph{random variable (RV)} is a function $X:\Omega\rightarrow\mathbb{R}$.
\end{definition}
\begin{remark}
	Any function $g\left(X\right)$ of a random variable is also a random
	variable (for instance $X^{2}$, $sgn \left(X\right)$, etc.).
\end{remark}
For a discrete $\Omega$ we define the \textbf{\textit{probability
		mass function (PMF)}} of $X$ (a \textit{discrete RV}) as
\[
\Dc\left(\left\{ X=x\right\} \right)=\sum_{\omega:X\left(\omega\right)=x}\Dc\left(\omega\right)
\]


Instead of writing $\Dc\left(\left\{ X=x\right\} \right)$  one sometimes writes $\Dc_X\left(\left\{ x\right\} \right)$ but more often,  simply $\Dc\left(x\right)$. This simplified notation, omitting any reference to the RV, is useful when the context is clear and we shall use it often. This omission is similar  to saying, for example,  "the probability of $(H,T)$ is 1/4" instead of  "the probability of getting $(H,T)$ by tossing a fair coin twice is 1/4".

\begin{example}
	A fair coin is tossed twice. The probability space is given by $\Omega=\left\{ T,H\right\} ^{2}$
	and  $\Dc\left(\omega\right)=\frac{1}{4}$ for any $\omega\in \Omega$. Let $X$ denote the number of heads obtained in each outcome ($\omega$). The possible values of $X$ are 0, 1, and 2,
	and
	\[
	\Dc\left(x\right)=\begin{cases}
		\frac{1}{4} & x=0,2\\
		\frac{1}{2} & x=1\\
		0 & \mbox{else}
	\end{cases}
	\]
	
\end{example}

For a continuous $\Omega$, we say that $X$ is a \textit{continuous
	RV} if there exists $f(x)\ge 0$ so that we can write, for every $D\subset\mathbb{R}$
\[
\Dc\left(X\in D\right)=\int_{D}f\left(x\right)dx
\]


In particular, this means that for any $a,b\in \mathbb{R}$, where $a\leq b$:
\[
\Dc\left(X\in\left[a,b\right]\right)=\int_{a}^{b}f\left(x\right)dx
\]

Again,  $f$ should actually be denoted by $f_X\left(x\right)$ to emphasize that it characterizes the RV $X$ but often the subscript $X$ is omitted.  In this course we assume that $f(x)$ is a regular function and therefore the probability mass function for a single point is 0: $\Dc\left(X=a\right)=\int_{a}^{a}f\left(x\right)dx=0$ for any $a\in\mathbb{R}$.

The function $f$ is called the \textbf{\textit{probability density
		function }}\textbf{(PDF)} of $X$. Note that -
\begin{itemize}
	\item $f\left(x\right)\ge0$
	\item $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}f\left(x\right)dx=1$
	\item $f\left(x\right)$ is a probability \emph{density}, not a probability. For example, it could be that $f\left(x\right)>1$.\\
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Mean and Variance}
\begin{definition}
	The \textbf{\textit{expected value}} of a random variable
	$X$ is
	$$
	\E  \left[X\right]=\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}x\Dc\left(x\right)\qquad\mbox{or}\qquad \E \left[X\right]=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}xf\left(x\right)dx
	$$
	
\end{definition}
Properties:
\begin{itemize}
	\item $\E \left[g\left(X\right)\right]=\sum g\left(x\right)\Dc\left(x\right)$,
	\item $\E \left[c\right]=c$
	\item Linear: $\E \left[aX+Y\right]=a\E \left[X\right]+\E \left[Y\right]$
	\item If $X$ and $Y$ are independent then:
	
	 $\E \left[XY\right]=\E \left[X\right]\E \left[Y\right]$
	since $\E [XY]=\sum\Dc(X=x,Y=y)x y  = \sum_{x,y}\Dc(x)\Dc(y)xy=\E [X]\E [Y]$.
\end{itemize}

\begin{definition}
	Let $X$ be a RV with $\E \left[X\right]=\mu$, then the \textbf{\textit{variance}}
	of $X$ is
	\[
	V\left(X\right)=\E \left[\left(X-\mu\right)^{2}\right]
	\]
	The \textbf{\textit{standard deviation}} is defined as $\sigma=\sqrt{V\left(X\right)}$.
\end{definition}
Properties of the variance:
\begin{itemize}
	\item $V(X)\geq0$ , ~$V(X)=0\,\iff\,X$ is a constant.
	\item $V\left(X\right)=\E \left[X^{2}\right] - (\E \left[X\right])^{2}$
	\item $V(aX+b)=a^{2}V(X)$
	\item $V(X+Y)=\E \left[\left(X+Y-\E (X)-\E (Y)\right)^{2}\right]=V(X)+V(Y)+2\overbrace{\E \left[\left(X-\E [X]\right)\left(Y-\E [Y]\right)\right]}^{\sigma \left(X,Y \right)}$
	\item If $X_{1},\dots,X_{n}$ are independent then $V\left(\sum X_{i}\right)=\sum V\left(X_{i}\right)$\end{itemize}

\begin{definition}
	The \textbf{\textit{covariance}} of $X$ and $Y$ is denoted by $\sigma \left(X,Y \right)$ or $\sigma_{XY}$ and is defined as
\end{definition}
$$
\sigma(X,Y)\equiv \sigma_{XY}\equiv \E \left[\left(X-\E [X]\right)\left(Y-\E [Y]\right)\right]=\E \left[XY\right]-\E [X]\E [Y]
$$

Properties:
\begin{itemize}
	\item $\sigma (X,Y)=\sigma \left(Y,X \right)$
	\item $\sigma \left(aX+b,cY+d \right) = a \cdot c \cdot\sigma \left(X,Y \right)$
	\item $\sigma(X,X)=V(X)$
\end{itemize}

Note the different meaning implied by the above definitions: The variance measures the variation of a single random variable (like the height of a person in a population), whereas covariance is a measure of how much two random variables  (like the height and weight) vary together.

\begin{example}
	Let $Z$ be a Bernoulli random variable. Calculate $Var[Z].$
	
Solution: $Var[Z] = \E [Z^2] - (\E [Z])^2 = p-p^2=p(1-p).$
\end{example}

\begin{example}
	Find the mean and variance of $X\sim U\left(\left[a,b\right]\right),$ where $U[a,b]$ is the  uniform distribution on $[a,b].$
	solution:
	
	$$
	\E \left[X\right]  =  \frac{1}{b-a}\int_{a}^{b} xdx=\frac{b+a}{2}
	$$
	$$
		\E \left[X^{2}\right]  = \frac{1}{b-a}\int_{a}^{b}x^2 dx =\frac{b^{2}+ab+a^{2}}{3}
	$$
	$$
		V\left(X\right)  =  \frac{b^{2}+ab+a^{2}}{3}-\frac{\left(b+a\right)^{2}}{4}=\frac{\left(b-a\right)^{2}}{12}
	$$

\end{example}

\subsection{A little Statistics: Mean and variance estimation}

This subject of this book is {\em Statistical} Machine Learning.  When we study {\em Probability} we assume a known distribution and calculate probability of events of interest. When we study {\em Statistics} we turn the question on its head: using samples from an unknown probability distribution, we try to {\em estimate} or {\em test} properties of the unknown distribution.

Consider, for example, the mean and the variance. The mean gives us a taste of around which point the data is distributed. The variance on the other hand gives us a taste of the concentration of the spread of the distribution around its mean. Low variance usually means that the distribution is highly concentrated around the mean. High variance usually means the opposite.

In a probability course, given that we know the distribution, we learned how to calculate its mean and variance.
Now we would like to ask a different question. We are given {\em samples} that were drawn from an unknown distribution.
Can we {\em estimate}  the distribution's mean and variance using only those samples?


Formally, let $X$ be a random variable, let $\Dc \left(x\right)$ be its (unknown) distribution, and let $X_1,\ldots,X_m\overset{i.i.d}{\sim} D(x)$, where i.i.d  denotes \emph{independent and identically distributed} random variables. We are given \emph{data}
samples: $x_1,\ldots,x_m$, where $x_i$ is a {\em number} - the sample obtained by sampling $X_i$.
%The data has its own mean and variance which are likely to be, at least, slightly different than those of $\Dc \left(x\right),$ and therefore it is customary, in statistics, to use different names for these.
In statistics, $\ensuremath{\mathbb{E}}[X]$ is called the {\em population} mean - "population" added to emphasize that it is the mean of the unknown distribution, not the mean of the observed data.
Similarly, $V(X)$ is called the \textit{population variance}.

There are many ways to estimate $\ensuremath{\mathbb{E}}[X]$ and $V(X)$ based on those $n$ samples, and each one is called an \textit{estimator}. Throughout the course we are going to use the following estimators (commonly denoted with the "hat" symbol to emphasize the fact that they are the way we choose to use the experimental observations in order to estimate the real, namely, the population, mean and variance):
\begin{itemize}
	\item Sample mean (an estimator for the population mean)
	$$		\hat{\mu}_X = \frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1}^m x_i$$
	\item Sample variance (an estimator for the population variance)
    $$		\hat{\sigma}_X^2 = \frac{1}{m-1} \sum_{i=1}^m (x_i-\hat{\mu}_X)^2$$
\end{itemize}

Whenever the context is clear, we will omit the subscript $X$ and write $\hat{\mu}$ and $\hat{\sigma}^2$ instead of $\hat{\mu}_X$ and $\hat{\sigma}_X^2$.
Note the $\frac{1}{m-1}$ factor. Why not use the observed variance of the samples,  $\frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1}^m (x_i-\hat{\mu}_X)^2$, as the population variance estimator? The answer is that if you calculate the expected value of these estimators  you will find (perhaps surprisingly) that $\ensuremath{\mathbb{E}}[\hat{\sigma}_X^2]=V(X)$ while $\ensuremath{\mathbb{E}}[\frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1}^m (x_i-\hat{\mu}_X)^2] = \frac{m-1}{m}V(X)$. The reason for that is that in general $\E(g(x))\neq g(\E(x))$ when $g(x)$ is not linear.
So, in expectation, $\hat{\sigma}_X^2$ is the one that gets the population variance exactly. If the expected value of an estimator is equal to the parameter it is designed to estimate,  that estimator is said to be {\em unbiased}. Therefore, although $\frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1}^m (x_i-\hat{\mu}_X)^2$ could have been considered, because of its form, a more natural way to estimate  $V(X)$, it is a \textit{biased} estimator. Biased estimators can be useful in certain cases, but for the mean and variance, we will stick with the unbiased ones.

\subsection{High dimensional distributions}
Going back to probability, we now consider random variables that instead of taking one real value take vector values in $\R ^d$, where $d\in \N $.

\subsubsection{Basic definitions}

\begin{definition}[Random vector]
	A  (column) 	\textbf{\textit{random vector}}: $\boldsymbol{X} = (X_1, \dots, X_n)^T$, is a finite collection of random variables, denoted $X_1, \dots, X_n$, defined on a common probability space $(\Omega, \Dc)$.
\end{definition}
It is customary to arrange the $X_i$'s in a column, which is why we define the random vector as a column  vector.
On the other hand, this makes inline writing less convenient, which is why we lay it down by writing it as a transpose of a row vector.

\begin{definition}[Joint distribution]
	Given random variables $X_1, \dots, X_n$ , that are defined on a probability space, the joint probability distribution for $X_1, \dots, X_n$ is a probability distribution that gives the probability that each of $X_1, \dots, X_n$ falls in any particular range (for continuous RVs) or discrete set (for discrete RVs) of values specified for that variable.
	
\end{definition}


\begin{definition}[Joint PDF of a random vector]
Two random variables $X_1$ and $X_2$ are \textbf{\textit{jointly continuous}} if there exists a non-negative function $f_{X_1,X_2}: \R ^2 \to \R $, such that, for any set $A \in \R ^2$, we have	
		$$
		\Dc\left( (X,Y) \in A \right) = \int_A f_{X_1,X_2}(x_1, x_2) dx_1 dx_2
		$$
		The function $f_{X_1,X_2}$ is called the 	\textbf{\textit{joint probability density function (JPDF)}} of $X_1$ and $X_2$. Often, if the context is clear, one omits the subscripts of $f$ and simply writes  $ f(x_1, x_2).$
		This definition can be generalized to any dimension.
		
\end{definition}


	
\begin{remark}[PDF for a random vector]
		Given $\boldsymbol{X} = (X_1, \dots, X_n)^T$, each of the scalar random variables $X_1, \dots, X_n$ can be characterized by its PDF. However, unless the scalar RV's are mutually independent, the PDF of each coordinate of a random vector does not completely describe the probabilistic behaviour of the whole vector. For instance, the behavior of the random vectors $\boldsymbol{X} = (X_1, X_2)$ and $\boldsymbol{\tilde{X}} = (X_1, X_1)$ is drastically different even if $X_1$ and $X_2$  have an identical PDF. To confirm this fact, take for example, $X_1, X_2 \sim U([-a, a])$ and $X_2 = - X_1$ and compare the probability to find $\boldsymbol{X}$ in the square $[0,1]\times [0,1]$ to that of finding $\boldsymbol{\tilde{X}}$  in the same square.
\end{remark}
	
\subsection{Normal Distribution}
As an example of random vectors, we now consider specific family of distributions - the Multivariate Normal (also called Multivariate Gaussian) Distributions.
%The normal distribution is very useful for many reasons. One reason is the Central Limit Theorem (CLT). You have likely seen the one-dimensional ("univariate") version of the CLT in probability course. In one of its forms, under some conditions, the CLT states that sample mean of random i.i.d variables (when properly scaled) converges in distribution to the normal distribution. There is a multivariate version of the CLT, which is one reason why you want to be familiar with the multivariate normal distribution.
The (multivariate) normal distribution is useful for a number of reasons, an important one being that many results and methods (such as propagation of uncertainty and least squares parameter fitting) can be derived analytically in explicit form when the relevant variables are (multivariate) normally distributed.

\begin{definition}[Univariate Normal Distribution] \label{def: UVN}
	A random variable x has a (one dimensional, $d=1$, or "univariate") 	\textbf{\textit{normal distribution}} with expectation $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$ if it has a PDF of the form:
	$$f(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2} } e^{ -\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2} } $$
	In this case we write: $x\sim\mathcal{N}({\mu},{\sigma^2})$
	
\end{definition}
\begin{definition}[Multivariate Normal Distribution] \label{def: MVN}
	a random vector $\mathbf{x}$ has a 	\textbf{\textit{multivariate normal distribution}} with expectation $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ and covariance matrix (see definition below) $C$ if it has a joint PDF of the form:
	
	$$f\left(\mathbf{x}\right)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\left(2\pi\right)^{n}\left|C\right|}}\exp\left\{ -\frac{\left(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}\right)^{T}\boldsymbol{C^{-1}}\left(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}\right)}{2}\right\} $$
	In this case we write: $\mathbf{x}\sim\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu},C)$
\end{definition}

In some cases we do not care about some of the variables in the distribution. For example, suppose we have joint probability function of the variables "height" and "weight" but we're only interested in one of them. This lead as to the following definition:

\begin{definition}[Marginal Distribution]
	\textbf{\textit{Marginal distribution}} of a subset of a collection of random variables is the probability distribution of the variables contained in the subset, no matter what the values of the other variables. It can be written as:
	$$f(\mathbf{x})=\int_{\mathbf{y}}f(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})\mathrm{d}\mathbf{y}$$
\end{definition}
where the $\mathbf{y}$ integration is over all the uninteresting RV's. Note that again we are writing for short $f(\mathbf{x})$ and $f(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})$  instead of  $f_{\mathbf{X}}(\mathbf{x})$ and $f_{\mathbf{X},\mathbf{Y}}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})$.

\begin{exercise}[Marginal of the 1st coordinate of a 2D normal RV with diagonal covariance matrix]
	Let $\mathbf{x}\sim\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu},C)$ where $\mathbf{x}=(x_1,x_2)$,  $\boldsymbol{\mu} = (\mu_1, \mu_2), C=\left(\begin{array}{cc}
		\sigma_{1}^{2} & 0\\
		0 & \sigma_{2}^{2}
	\end{array}\right)$.
	
	Find the PDF of the marginal distribution of $x_1$.
\end{exercise}
\noindent
\textbf{Solution:}  Observe that we have
\begin{align*}
	& \frac{1}{\sqrt{\left(2\pi\right)^{n}\left|C\right|}}\exp\left\{ -\frac{\left(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}\right)^{T}C^{-1}\left(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}\right)}{2}\right\} \\ &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{\left(2\pi\right)^{2}\left|\left(\begin{array}{cc}
				\sigma_{1}^{2} & 0\\
				0 & \sigma_{2}^{2}
			\end{array}\right)\right|}}\exp\left\{ -\frac{\left(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}\right)^{T}\left(\begin{array}{cc}
			\sigma_{1}^{-2} & 0\\
			0 & \sigma_{2}^{-2}
		\end{array}\right)\left(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}\right)}{2}\right\}\\&
	= \frac{1}{\sqrt{\left(2\pi\right)^{2}\sigma_{1}^{2}\sigma_{2}^{2}}}\exp\left\{ -\frac{\left(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}\right)^{T}\left(\begin{array}{cc}
			\sigma_{1}^{-2} & 0\\
			0 & \sigma_{2}^{-2}
		\end{array}\right)\left(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}\right)}{2}\right\} \\&
	= \frac{1}{\sqrt{\left(2\pi\right)^{2}\sigma_{1}^{2}\sigma_{2}^{2}}}\exp\left\{ -\frac{1}{2} \left(\frac{x_{1}-\mu_{1}}{\sigma_{1}}\right)^{2} - \frac{1}{2} \left(\frac{x_{2} - \mu_{2}}{\sigma_{2}}\right)^{2}\right\} \\&
	=	\frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi\sigma_{1}^{2}}}\exp\left\{ -\frac{1}{2} \left(\frac{x_{1}-\mu_{1}}{\sigma_{1}}\right)^{2}\right\} \frac{1}{\sqrt{\left(2\pi\right)\sigma_{2}^{2}}}\exp\left\{ -\frac{1}{2} \left(\frac{x_{2}-\mu_{2}}{\sigma_{2}}\right)^{2}\right\}
\end{align*}

Now, since: $\int_{-\infty}^\infty\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma_{2}^{2}}}\exp\left\{ -\frac{1}{2} \left(\frac{x_{2}-\mu_{2}}{\sigma_{2}}\right)^{2}\right\}
\mathrm{d}x_2=1$, we get:

\begin{align*}
	&\int_{-\infty}^\infty\frac{1}{\sqrt{\left(2\pi\right)^{n}\left|C\right|}}\exp\left\{ -\frac{\left(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}\right)^{T}C^{-1}\left(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}\right)}{2}\right\}\;dx_2 \\
	&=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma_{1}^{2}}}\exp\left\{ -\frac{1}{2} \left(\frac{x_{1}-\mu_{1}}{\sigma_{1}}\right)^{2}\right\}
\end{align*}

and by definition \ref{def: UVN} we get:
$x_1\sim\mathcal{N}({\mu_1},{\sigma_1^2})$

\subsection{Covariance Matrix}

Recall the population variance $V(X)$ of a scalar (one dimensional) random variable $X$. What is the proper generalization for the case of a random vector?
\begin{definition}[Covariance Matrix]
	Consider a $d$-dimensional random vector $ \mathbf{X} =(X_{1},X_{2},...,X_d)^T$.
	We define the  {\em Covariance Matrix} $C$ as the $d \times d$ matrix whose $ (i,j)$ entry is the covariance $C_{ij}=\sigma (X_i,X_j)$
	\begin{itemize}
		\item In other words:
		\begingroup
		\footnotesize % \small in 11pt base font is 10pt
		$$C=\left(\begin{array}{ccc}
			\mathbb{E}\left[\left(X_{1}-\mathbb{E}\left[X_{1}\right]\right)\left(X_{1}-\mathbb{E}\left[X_{1}\right]\right)\right] & ... & \mathbb{E}\left[\left(X_{1}-\mathbb{E}\left[X_{1}\right]\right)\left(X_{d}-\mathbb{E}\left[X_{d}\right]\right)\right]\\
			\vdots & \ddots & \vdots\\
			\mathbb{E}\left[\left(X_{d}-\mathbb{E}\left[X_{d}\right]\right)\left(X_{1}-\mathbb{E}\left[X_{1}\right]\right)\right] & ... & \mathbb{E}\left[\left(X_{d}-\mathbb{E}\left[X_{d}\right]\right)\left(X_{d}-\mathbb{E}\left[X_{d}\right]\right)\right]
		\end{array}\right)$$
		\endgroup
		
		\item Recall that we can use  matrix notations for random vectors. In matrix notation,
		$$C=\mathbb{E}[({X} - {\mathbb{E}\left[X\right]} )({X} -{\mathbb{E}\left[X\right]} )^{T}]$$
		
		\item The diagonal elements of $C$ are $\sigma (X_i, X_i) \equiv \sigma^2 _{X_i} \equiv  V(X_i)$. $C$ is symmetric positive semidefinite.
		
	\end{itemize}
	
\end{definition}

\subsection{Estimating the Covariance Matrix}

Back to statistics, let us consider how to estimate the covariance matrix based on random sampling. In this context, $C$ is called the {\em population covariance matrix}.
Let's start with the case $d=2$. Consider a two-dimensional random vector $\mathbf{X}=(X_1,X_2)$, where, for example, $X_1$ is the height of a person and $X_2$ is the weight.

Taking a sample of $m$ people from the population, we equip the data elements with two indices, the first stands for the feature (1 for height, 2 for weight) and the second for the person's designated number. 
So the height samples are denoted by  $x_{1,1},..,x_{1,m}$ and the corresponding weight samples are $x_{2,1},..,x_{2,m}$.
Note that we can write the data as a  matrix $\mathbf{X}\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times m}$ where $m$ is the {\em sample size} and $d$ is the dimension (number of random variables).
In our case, $d=2$ and
$$\mathbf{X} = 
\left(\begin{array}{ccc}
	x_{1,1} & ... & x_{1,m}\\
		x_{2,1} & ... & x_{2,m}
\end{array}\right)
$$

Generalizing the estimators we chose for a one-dimensional RV we now define the \textbf{\textit{sample covariance}} by
$$\hat{\sigma}(X_i, X_j) = \frac{1}{m-1} \sum^{m}_{k=1}{(x_{i,k}-\hat{\mu}_i)(x_{j,k}-\hat{\mu}_j)}, ~ ~ i,j=1,2 $$
where $\hat{\mu}_i$ is the sample mean of the random variable $X_i$.
In particular, the  variance estimators, that is, the sample variances of the heights and weights are:
$$ \hat{\sigma}\left(X_i,X_i\right)  = \frac{1}{m-1} \sum^{m}_{k=1}(x_{i,k} - \hat{\mu}_i)^2, i=1,2 \\$$



where $\hat{\sigma}\left(X_i,X_i\right)\equiv \hat{\sigma}^2_{X_i} \equiv  \hat{\sigma}^2_i \equiv \hat{\sigma}_{i,i}$ are all equivalent notations. We can now define the sample covariance matrix $\hat{C}$. Again, as customary in statistics, we use the "hat notation" which reminds that $\hat{C}$ is our prescription for estimating the population covariance matrix $C$, based on the data we have.
\vspace{3mm}

\begin{definition}[Sample Covariance Matrix]
Consider a $d$-dimensional random vector $\textbf{X}=(X_1,\ldots,X_d)$. Suppose that we have $m$ i.i.d samples $\mathbf{x}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{x}_m$ of $\mathbf{X}$. Here, the sample $\mathbf{x}_i$ is a vector in $\R^d$, and the boldface notation reminds us that $\mathbf{x}_i$ is a vector, not a scalar.

The sample covariance matrix of $\mathbf{x}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{x}_d$ is defined to be the square $d$-by-$d$ matrix whose elements are given by $\hat{C}_{i,j}=\hat{\sigma}\left(X_{i},X_{j}\right)$. 
\end{definition}

The sample covariance matrix is symmetric since $\hat{\sigma}\left(X_{j},X_{i}\right)=\hat{\sigma}\left(X_{i},X_{j}\right)$. The diagonal entries of the sample covariance matrix are the sample variances and the other entries are the sample covariances.
In matrix notation, the sample covariance matrix is given by
$$\hat{C} = \frac{1}{m-1} \sum^{m}_{i=1}(\mathbf{x}_i-\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}})(\mathbf{x}_i-\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}}^T)$$


where $\boldsymbol{\mu} \in\mathbb{R}^{d\times 1}$ is a column vector of the sample means (in the $d=2$ case, $\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}}^T = (\hat{\mu}_1, \hat{\mu}_2)$).
% Depending on the context, $\bar{X}$ may also denote the means matrix: $\bar{X} \in\mathbb{R}^{d\times m}$ with elements $\bar{X}_{i,j}=\hat{\mu}_i$.
If $\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}}=0$, that is, if the data is shifted to have zero mean, the covariance matrix is easily computed from the symmetric matrix $XX^T$, using the relation $\hat{C} = \frac{XX^T}{m-1}$.

\begin{exercise}
	let $X^T=\left(\begin{array}{cc}
		150 & 45\\
		170 & 74\\
		184 & 79
	\end{array}\right)$ be samples of height and weight of 3 different people where $X_{1, i}$ is the height and $X_{2, i}$ is the weight of the i'th person. Calculate the covariance matrix of the sample.
\end{exercise}
\textbf{Solution:} First of all we will make the data centered by subtracting the corresponding sample mean from each entry of $X^T$.
%matrix $(1,1,1)^T\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}}^T$:
We have: $\hat{\mu}_1=168, \hat{\mu}_2=66$, so
$$X_{centered}^{T}=X^T  - \left(\begin{array}{cc}
	168 & 66\\
	168 & 66\\
	168 & 66
\end{array}
\right)
=\left(\begin{array}{cc}
	150 & 45\\
	170 & 74\\
	184 & 79
\end{array}\right) - \left(\begin{array}{cc}
	168 & 66\\
	168 & 66\\
	168 & 66
\end{array}
\right)
=\left(\begin{array}{cc}
	-18 & -21\\
	2 & 8\\
	16 & 13
\end{array}\right)$$
Now,
$$
\hat{C} = \frac{X_{centered}X_{centered}^{T}}{3-1}=\left(\begin{array}{cc}
	292 & 301\\
	301 & 337
\end{array}\right)
$$
