\section{Probability and Statistics}

\subsection{Introduction (Optional section)}

We are preparing a tea party for $N$ guests.
Let us denote the average number of tea cups each participant would like to drink by $\mu_{tea}$.
In statistics, this average is called the \textit{population mean}, meaning simply that it refers to an average over the whole group which is under consideration -
 all the $N$ guests in our case.
 \vspace{3mm}

The total number of tea cups we should prepare is $T=N \times\mu_{tea}$. We do not know $\mu_{tea}$ and
it would take too long to ask each of the guests how many tea cups they would like to have.
So instead, we \textit{sample independently} people in the guest list: we randomly choose one, call and ask how many tea cups they would like to drink and write down the answer as $x_1$. We then randomly call another one (could accidentally be the same one) and write the second answer as $x_2$. We continue $m$ times.
To estimate the population mean, $\mu_{tea}$, we use  the average of the answers which in statistics is called the \textit{sample mean} and is given by
$$\hat{\mu}_{tea} \equiv \frac{1}{m}\sum_{k=1}^{m}x_k.$$
Finally, we \textit{predict} that we will need $T= N \times \hat{\mu}_{tea}$ cups.

The little "hat" symbol, $\hat{}$ , is there to point out that the right hand side of the above definition is what we \textit{choose} as an \textit{estimator} for the population mean $\mu_{tea}$.  The linearity of the expectation value ensures that the expectation value of the estimator $\hat{\mu}_{tea}$ is indeed equal to   $\mu_{tea}$. An estimator whose expectation value is equal to the quantity it is designed to estimate, is called an \textit{unbiased} estimator.
$\hat{\mu}_{tea}$ may seem an obvious choice since it has a form similar to that of $\mu_{tea}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{k=1}^{N}x_k$.
However, if we tried to estimate the \textit{variance}, $V(x)$, of the personal tea consumption among the guests using the same logic, that is, by choosing $\frac{1}{m}\sum_{k=1}^{m}(x_k-\hat{\mu}_{tea})^2$ as an estimator, we would find, perhaps surprisingly, that we are consistently off target:
the expectation value of $\frac{1}{m}\sum_{k=1}^{m}(x_k-\hat{\mu}_{tea})^2$ is \textit{different} than the variance $V(x)$ and therefore $\frac{1}{m}\sum_{k=1}^{m}(x_k-\hat{\mu}_{tea})^2$ is a \textit{biased} estimator.
We will return to this point in the next sections.
\vspace{3mm}

The above way in which we predicted $T$ can be formulated as the following \textit{learning algorithm}:
\begin{itemize}
	\item Enter an integer $m<N$
	\item Set $k=1$ and $\hat{\mu}_{tea}=0$
	\item For $k\le m$, set $x_k = \text{answer from guest number \textit{k}}$
	\item Set $\hat{\mu}_{tea}=\frac{x_k+(k-1)\hat{\mu}_{tea}}{k}$ and $k=k+1$
	\item Once $k=m$, return $T=N\times \hat{\mu}_{tea}$
\end{itemize}

We call this algorithm a learning algorithm because we learn from our experience: each time we ask another person $\hat{\mu}_{tea}$ changes - \textit{probably} getting closer to $\mu_{tea}$ - and therefore also our prediction of $T$ improve. Note that we might get unlucky and ask a non-representative set of $m$ people that particularly drink much more or much less than the average guest, so  $\hat{\mu}_{tea}$ might be a bad approximation of $\mu_{tea}$.
To not disappoint many of the guests on one hand and to not prepare too much tea cups on the other hand, we can require that our algorithm will keep running until, for example, it becomes \textit{probable} that $\hat{\mu}_{tea}$ is accurate enough. For example, we can choose an accuracy parameter $\epsilon>0$ and require that $|\hat{\mu}_{tea}-\mu_{tea}|<\epsilon$.
The question is how many people we should ask to ensure this requirement holds?

If we knew  the \textit{probability distribution} (or more precisely, since we are dealing with discrete numbers here, the \textit{probability mass function}) of tea cup consumption among our guests, we could calculate the answer to that question. However, even without knowing the exact \textit{probability distribution} we can still adapt  very general assumptions about its properties, such as its variance, in order to get an upper bounds for the probability that our error will be bigger than $\epsilon$.

\vspace{3mm}
The above algorithm is a learning algorithms that can obviously be implemented by a traditional computer program.
In many cases, the amount of data generated by the modern abundance of computerized sensors and memory is vast as well as too multi-dimensional, involving \textit{random vectors} with thousands of components and not only a single random variable such as the number of tea cups. Such data must be processed by specially designed algorithms, called machine learning algorithms, but the main principles demonstrated in above still remain: Choose estimators (that can be very complicated and may depend on many variables) in order to make predictions and take enough samples to be sure that, with high probability, the errors are within a required range. As seen from the above example,  probabilistic and statisticlal concepts take important part in such algorithm design and therefore we will now make a short review of those.


\subsection{Probability Space}
The basic object in probability theory is that of a \emph{probability space} which consists of two ingredients: the \textit{sample space} and the \textit{probability function}.
\begin{definition}
A \emph{sample space} $\Omega$ is a set that contains all possible outcomes. $\omega\in\Omega$ denotes a single outcome.
\end{definition}
Examples:
\begin{itemize}
\item Coin toss: $\Omega=\left\{ H,T\right\} $
\item Three coin tosses $\Omega=\left\{ H,T\right\}^3 $
\item Toss a coin until heads comes up: $\Omega=\left\{ T^{n-1}H:n\in\N\right\} $
\item The number of times each of the 1000 product web-pages in an online store is viewed by an online customer
 $\Omega=\{ n_1, n_2,....,n_{1000}\} $ with $n_i \in \N$
\item The number of times each of the 1000 product web-pages in an online store is viewed by an online customer
and the number of each product that eventually is bought: $\Omega=\{ n_1, n_2,....,n_{1000}\} \times \{ N_1, N_2,...N_{1000} \}$ with $n_i, N_i \in \N$
\item Height, weight, blood pressure, sex of patients with a medical condition and the success or failure of an experimental drug  : $\Omega=[0,\infty)\times [0,\infty) \times[0,\infty) \times \{M,F\}\times \{Yes,No\} $
\end{itemize}
\vspace{3mm}

Suppose someone tossed a coin three times and told us that the result contained more heads than tails. This information does not define a unique element in $\Omega$, but it tells us that the outcome is an element in the subset $\left\{ (H,H,H),(H,H,T),(H,T,H),(T,H,H)\right\} $.
\begin{definition}
An \emph{event} $A$ is any subset of possible outcomes, $A\subseteq\Omega$.
\end{definition}
Quick reminder:
\begin{itemize}
\item $A^{c}$ (or sometimes $\overline{A}$) denotes the \textit{complement} of $A$, $A^{c}=\Omega\backslash A$ . In other words, $A^{c}$ is the event "$A$ did not occur".
\item We say that $A$ and $B$ are \textit{disjoint events} if $A\cap B=\emptyset$.
\item $\Omega$ and $\emptyset$ are also events. If $A$ and $B$ are two events, then $A\cup B$ is also an event, and so is $A\cap B$.
\end{itemize}

Intuitively, a probability function assigns to an event a number which quantifies our knowledge or belief about the possibility of observing any outcome in that event.


\begin{definition}
	A \textit{probability space} is a tuple \footnote{For our needs this simple and intuitive definition is sufficient, though, richer definitions exist.} $(\Omega, \Dc)$ where $\Omega$ is a \emph{sample space} and $\Dc:2^{\Omega}\rightarrow\R$ is a probability function such that
	\begin{enumerate}
		\item $\Dc(\Omega)=1$
		\item for all $\omega\in\Omega, \Dc(\omega)\in[0,1]$
		\item for all $A,B\subseteq\Omega$ such that $A\cap B=\emptyset,$ we have $\Dc(A \cup B)=\Dc(A)+\Dc(B).$
	\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\begin{example}
	Suppose we throw two fair dice, then $\Omega=\left\{ 1,\dots,6\right\} ^{2}$ and $\Dc\left(\left(i,j\right)\right)=\frac{1}{36}$
\end{example}

\begin{exercise}
	For all $A,B\subseteq\Omega$: $\Dc(A\cup B)=\Dc(A)+\Dc(B)-\Dc(A\cap B)$
\end{exercise}
Solution:\\
$A\cup B=(A\setminus B)\cup(B\setminus A)\cup(A\cap B)\; ,A=(A\setminus B)\cup(A\cap B)$ and $B=(B\setminus A)\cup(A\cap B).$
$$\Dc(A\cup B)=\Dc(A\setminus B)+\Dc(B\setminus A)+\Dc(A\cap B)=\Dc(A)-\Dc(A\cap B)+\Dc(B) -  \cancel{\Dc(A\cap B)}  + \cancel{\Dc(A\cap B)} $$

\begin{definition}
	$A,B\subseteq\Omega$ are called \emph{independent} if the occurrence of one does not affect the probability of occurrence of the other. Consequently: $$\Dc(A\cap B) =\Dc(A)\cdot\Dc(B).$$
\end{definition}

\begin{exercise}
	Show that if $A$ and $B$ are independent then $A$ and $B^c$ are also independent.
\end{exercise}
Solution: $\Dc(A)=\Dc(A\cap B)+\Dc(A\cap B^c)\Rightarrow\Dc(A\cap B^c)=\Dc(A)(1-\Dc(B))=\Dc(A)\cdot\Dc(B^c).$\\


\begin{theorem}[The union bound] Let 	$(\Omega, \Dc)$ be a probability space. The probability function is \emph{sub-additive}, i.e., for any sequence $(A_k)$ of events,
	\[
	\Dc(\cup_{k=1}^\infty A_k) \le \sum_{k=1} ^\infty \Dc(A_k)
	\]
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
	Let $B_1=A_1$. For each $k \in \{2,3,\ldots\}$, let $B_k = A_k
	\setminus \cup_{i=1}^{k-1} A_i$. Note that the $B_1...B_k$ are disjoint, and
	$\cup_{i=1}^k A_i=\cup_{i=1}^k B_i$.
	Also, since $B_k\subseteq A_k$,
	$\Dc(B_k) \le \Dc(A_k)$
	for every $k \in \N$. It follows that
	\[
	\Dc(\cup_{k=1}^\infty A_k) = \Dc(\cup_{k=1}^\infty B_k) = \sum_{k=1}
	^\infty \Dc(B_k) \le \sum_{k=1}
	^\infty \Dc(A_k)~.
	\]
\end{proof}


\subsection{Random Variables, discrete and continuous}

So far we have asked questions like: does an event happen or not and what is the probability of the event. But what about different questions, such as, "how much." For example, if we get a dollar if a coin flip turns head, we can ask how many dollars would we get after 3 rounds?  In order to answer this question, we should identify the sample space, which is
$\Omega=\{HHH,HHT,HTH,THH,HTT,THT,TTH,TTT\}$. Then we would need to define a reward function that will quantify ones's profit for each outcome of the experiment ($\omega\in \Omega$):
$X(HHH)=3,X(HHT)=2,$ and so on. This quantifying function $X$ is called a \emph{random variable} (RV).
\begin{definition}
	Given a probability space $\left(\Omega,\Dc\right)$, a real-valued \emph{random variable (RV)} is a function $X:\Omega\rightarrow\mathbb{R}$.
\end{definition}
\begin{remark}
	Any function $g\left(X\right)$ of a random variable is also a random
	variable (for instance $X^{2}$, $sgn \left(X\right)$, etc.).
\end{remark}
For a discrete $\Omega$ we define the \textbf{\textit{probability
		mass function (PMF)}} of $X$ (a \textit{discrete RV}) as
\[
\Dc\left(\left\{ X=x\right\} \right)=\sum_{\omega:X\left(\omega\right)=x}\Dc\left(\omega\right)
\]


Instead of writing $\Dc\left(\left\{ X=x\right\} \right)$  one sometimes writes $\Dc_X\left(\left\{ x\right\} \right)$ but more often,  simply $\Dc\left(x\right)$. This simplified notation, omitting any reference to the RV, is useful when the context is clear and we shall use it often. This omission is similar  to saying, for example,  "the probability of $(H,T)$ is 1/4" instead of  "the probability of getting $(H,T)$ by tossing a fair coin twice is 1/4".

\begin{example}
	A fair coin is tossed twice. The probability space is given by $\Omega=\left\{ T,H\right\} ^{2}$
	and  $\Dc\left(\omega\right)=\frac{1}{4}$ for any $\omega\in \Omega$. Let $X$ denote the number of heads obtained in each outcome ($\omega$). The possible values of $X$ are 0, 1, and 2,
	and
	\[
	\Dc\left(x\right)=\begin{cases}
		\frac{1}{4} & x=0,2\\
		\frac{1}{2} & x=1\\
		0 & \mbox{else}
	\end{cases}
	\]
	
\end{example}

For a continuous $\Omega$, we say that $X$ is a \textit{continuous
	RV} if there exists $f(x)\ge 0$ so that we can write, for every $D\subset\mathbb{R}$
\[
\Dc\left(X\in D\right)=\int_{D}f\left(x\right)dx
\]


In particular, this means that for any $a,b\in \mathbb{R}$, where $a\leq b$:
\[
\Dc\left(X\in\left[a,b\right]\right)=\int_{a}^{b}f\left(x\right)dx
\]

Again,  $f$ should actually be denoted by $f_X\left(x\right)$ to emphasize that it characterizes the RV $X$ but often the subscript $X$ is omitted.  In this course we assume that $f(x)$ is a regular function and therefore the probability mass function for a single point is 0: $\Dc\left(X=a\right)=\int_{a}^{a}f\left(x\right)dx=0$ for any $a\in\mathbb{R}$.

The function $f$ is called the \textbf{\textit{probability density
		function }}\textbf{(PDF)} of $X$. Note that -
\begin{itemize}
	\item $f\left(x\right)\ge0$
	\item $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}f\left(x\right)dx=1$
	\item $f\left(x\right)$ is a probability \emph{density}, not a probability. For example, it could be that $f\left(x\right)>1$.\\
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Mean and Variance}
\begin{definition}
	The \textbf{\textit{expected value}} of a random variable
	$X$ is
	$$
	\E  \left[X\right]=\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}x\Dc\left(x\right)\qquad\mbox{or}\qquad \E \left[X\right]=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}xf\left(x\right)dx
	$$
	
\end{definition}
Properties:
\begin{itemize}
	\item $\E \left[g\left(X\right)\right]=\sum g\left(x\right)\Dc\left(x\right)$,
	\item $\E \left[c\right]=c$
	\item Linear: $\E \left[aX+Y\right]=a\E \left[X\right]+\E \left[Y\right]$
	\item If $X$ and $Y$ are independent then:
	
	 $\E \left[XY\right]=\E \left[X\right]\E \left[Y\right]$
	since $\E [XY]=\sum\Dc(X=x,Y=y)x y  = \sum_{x,y}\Dc(x)\Dc(y)xy=\E [X]\E [Y]$.
\end{itemize}

\begin{definition}
	Let $X$ be a RV with $\E \left[X\right]=\mu$, then the \textbf{\textit{variance}}
	of $X$ is
	\[
	V\left(X\right)=\E \left[\left(X-\mu\right)^{2}\right]
	\]
	The \textbf{\textit{standard deviation}} is defined as $\sigma=\sqrt{V\left(X\right)}$.
\end{definition}
Properties of the variance:
\begin{itemize}
	\item $V(X)\geq0$ , ~$V(X)=0\,\iff\,X$ is a constant.
	\item $V\left(X\right)=\E \left[X^{2}\right] - (\E \left[X\right])^{2}$
	\item $V(aX+b)=a^{2}V(X)$
	\item $V(X+Y)=\E \left[\left(X+Y-\E (X)-\E (Y)\right)^{2}\right]=V(X)+V(Y)+2\overbrace{\E \left[\left(X-\E [X]\right)\left(Y-\E [Y]\right)\right]}^{\sigma \left(X,Y \right)}$
	\item If $X_{1},\dots,X_{n}$ are independent then $V\left(\sum X_{i}\right)=\sum V\left(X_{i}\right)$\end{itemize}

\begin{definition}
	The \textbf{\textit{covariance}} of $X$ and $Y$ is denoted by $\sigma \left(X,Y \right)$ or $\sigma_{XY}$ and is defined as
\end{definition}
$$
\sigma(X,Y)\equiv \sigma_{XY}\equiv \E \left[\left(X-\E [X]\right)\left(Y-\E [Y]\right)\right]=\E \left[XY\right]-\E [X]\E [Y]
$$

Properties:
\begin{itemize}
	\item $\sigma (X,Y)=\sigma \left(Y,X \right)$
	\item $\sigma \left(aX+b,cY+d \right) = a \cdot c \cdot\sigma \left(X,Y \right)$
	\item $\sigma(X,X)=V(X)$
\end{itemize}

Note the different meaning implied by the above definitions: The variance measures the variation of a single random variable (like the height of a person in a population), whereas covariance is a measure of how much two random variables  (like the height and weight) vary together.

\begin{example}
	Let $Z$ be a Bernoulli random variable. Calculate $Var[Z].$
	
Solution: $Var[Z] = \E [Z^2] - (\E [Z])^2 = p-p^2=p(1-p).$
\end{example}

\begin{example}
	Find the mean and variance of $X\sim U\left(\left[a,b\right]\right),$ where $U[a,b]$ is the  uniform distribution on $[a,b].$
	solution:
	
	$$
	\E \left[X\right]  =  \frac{1}{b-a}\int_{a}^{b} xdx=\frac{b+a}{2}
	$$
	$$
		\E \left[X^{2}\right]  = \frac{1}{b-a}\int_{a}^{b}x^2 dx =\frac{b^{2}+ab+a^{2}}{3}
	$$
	$$
		V\left(X\right)  =  \frac{b^{2}+ab+a^{2}}{3}-\frac{\left(b+a\right)^{2}}{4}=\frac{\left(b-a\right)^{2}}{12}
	$$

\end{example}

\subsection{A little Statistics: Mean and variance estimation}

This subject of this book is {\em Statistical} Machine Learning.  When we study {\em Probability} we assume a known distribution and calculate probability of events of interest. When we study {\em Statistics} we turn the question on its head: using samples from an unknown probability distribution, we try to {\em estimate} or {\em test} properties of the unknown distribution.

Consider, for example, the mean and the variance. The mean gives us a taste of around which point the data is distributed. The variance on the other hand gives us a taste of the concentration of the spread of the distribution around its mean. Low variance usually means that the distribution is highly concentrated around the mean. High variance usually means the opposite.

In a probability course, given that we know the distribution, we learned how to calculate its mean and variance.
Now we would like to ask a different question. We are given {\em samples} that were drawn from an unknown distribution.
Can we {\em estimate}  the distribution's mean and variance using only those samples?


Formally, let $X$ be a random variable, let $\Dc \left(x\right)$ be its (unknown) distribution, and let $X_1,\ldots,X_m\overset{i.i.d}{\sim} D(x)$, where i.i.d  denotes \emph{independent and identically distributed} random variables. We are given \emph{data samples}: $x_1,\ldots,x_m$, where each $x_i$ is a {\em number} obtained by sampling $X_i$.
%The data has its own mean and variance which are likely to be, at least, slightly different than those of $\Dc \left(x\right),$ and therefore it is customary, in statistics, to use different names for these.
In statistics, $\ensuremath{\mathbb{E}}[X]$ is called the {\em population} mean - "population" added to emphasize that it is the mean of the unknown distribution, not the mean of the observed data.
Similarly, $V(X)$ is called the \textit{population variance}.

There are many ways to estimate $\ensuremath{\mathbb{E}}[X]$ and $V(X)$ based on those $n$ samples, and each one is called an \textit{estimator}. Throughout this book we are going to use the following estimators (commonly denoted with the "hat" symbol to emphasize the fact that they are the way we choose to use the experimental observations in order to estimate the real, namely, the population, mean and variance):
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{\textit{Sample mean}} (an estimator for the population mean)
	$$		\hat{\mu}_X = \frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1}^m x_i$$
	\item \textbf{\textit{Sample variance}} (an estimator for the population variance)
    $$		\hat{\sigma}_X^2 = \frac{1}{m-1} \sum_{i=1}^m (x_i-\hat{\mu}_X)^2$$
\end{itemize}

Whenever the context is clear, we will omit the subscript $X$ and write $\hat{\mu}$ and $\hat{\sigma}^2$ instead of $\hat{\mu}_X$ and $\hat{\sigma}_X^2$.
Note that the sample variance is somewhat different from the variance of the $x_i$'s because of the division by $m-1$ instead of $m$. 

\begin{exercise}
Show that the expectation values of the above estimators equal the parameters there are designed to estimate, namely the population mean and the population variance.

\textbf{Solution}. Taking the expectation value of the sample mean one gets
$$
\E\left(\hat{\mu}_X\right) = \frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1}^m \E(x_i) = \E(X) \frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1}^m =  \E(X)= \mu_X
$$ 
where we used the linearity property of the expectation value. Taking the expectation value of the sample variance one gets
$$
\E\left(\hat{\sigma}_X^2\right) =\frac{1}{m-1} \sum_{i=1}^m \E\left((x_i-\hat{\mu}_X)^2\right)=\frac{1}{m-1} \sum_{i=1}^m \E\left(x_i^2-2x_i\frac{1}{m} \sum_{i'=1}^m x_{i'}+\frac{1}{m^2} \sum_{i,i'=1}^m x_ix_{i'}\right)
$$
The $X_i$'s are i.i.d which implies that  $\E(x_i)=\E(X)$ and $\E(x_i^2)=\E(X^2)$ for every $i$, as well as that   $\E(x_ix_{i'})=\E^2(X)=\mu^2_X$ for every  $i\neq i'$. Substituting into the above sums one obtains  
$$
\E\left(\hat{\sigma}_X^2\right) =\E(X^2) -\E^2(X)=V(X)= \sigma^2_X
$$
\end{exercise}

\begin{definition}
	If the expected value of an estimator is equal to the parameter it is designed to estimate,  that estimator is said to be \textbf{\textit{ unbiased}}. Otherwise, it is said to be \textbf{\textit{biased}}.
\end{definition}

As seen from the above exercise,  $\hat{\mu}_X$ is an unbiased estimator of the population mean, $\mu_X$, and $\hat{\sigma}_X^2$ is an unbiased estimator of the population variance $\sigma^2_X$.  
We also see that the expected value of  the variance of the $x_i$'s, $\frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1}^m (x_i-\hat{\mu}_X)^2$, is $\frac{m-1}{m}\sigma^2_X\neq \sigma^2_X$, that is, although the variance of the sample looks, in form, more similar to that of $\sigma^2_X$ the sample variance, it is a \textit{biased} estimator of $\sigma^2_X$. The reason for that is that in general $\E(g(x))\neq g(\E(x))$ when $g(x)$ is not linear, which is exactly the case here.
\vspace{3mm} 

We should however note that biased estimators can be useful in certain cases, but for the mean and variance, we will stick with the unbiased ones.

\subsection{High dimensional distributions}
Going back to probability, we now consider random variables that instead of taking one real value take vector values in $\R ^d$, where $d\in \N $.

\subsubsection{Basic definitions}

\begin{definition}[Random vector]
	A  (column) 	\textbf{\textit{random vector}}: $\boldsymbol{X} = (X_1, \dots, X_n)^T$, is a finite collection of random variables, denoted $X_1, \dots, X_n$, defined on a common probability space $(\Omega, \Dc)$.
\end{definition}
It is customary to arrange the $X_i$'s in a column, which is why we define the random vector as a column  vector.
On the other hand, this makes inline writing less convenient, which is why we lay it down by writing it as a transpose of a row vector.

\begin{definition}[Joint distribution]
	Given random variables $X_1, \dots, X_n$ , that are defined on a probability space, the joint probability distribution for $X_1, \dots, X_n$ is a probability distribution that gives the probability that each of $X_1, \dots, X_n$ falls in any particular range (for continuous RVs) or discrete set (for discrete RVs) of values specified for that variable.
	
\end{definition}


\begin{definition}[Joint PDF of a random vector]
Two random variables $X_1$ and $X_2$ are \textbf{\textit{jointly continuous}} if there exists a non-negative function $f_{X_1,X_2}: \R ^2 \to \R $, such that, for any set $A \in \R ^2$, we have	
		$$
		\Dc\left( (X,Y) \in A \right) = \int_A f_{X_1,X_2}(x_1, x_2) dx_1 dx_2
		$$
		The function $f_{X_1,X_2}$ is called the 	\textbf{\textit{joint probability density function (JPDF)}} of $X_1$ and $X_2$. Often, if the context is clear, one omits the subscripts of $f$ and simply writes  $ f(x_1, x_2).$
		This definition can be generalized to any dimension.
		
\end{definition}


	
\begin{remark}
		Given $\boldsymbol{X} = (X_1, \dots, X_n)^T$, each of the scalar random variables $X_1, \dots, X_n$ can be characterized by its PDF. However, unless the scalar RV's are mutually independent, the PDF of each coordinate of a random vector does not completely describe the probabilistic behavior of the whole vector. For instance, the behavior of the random vectors $\boldsymbol{X} = (X_1, X_2)$ and $\boldsymbol{\tilde{X}} = (X_1, X_1)$ is drastically different even if $X_1$ and $X_2$  have an identical PDF. To confirm this fact, take for example, $X_1, X_2 \sim U([-a, a])$ and $X_2 = - X_1$ and compare the probability to find $\boldsymbol{X}$ in the square $[0,1]\times [0,1]$ to that of finding $\boldsymbol{\tilde{X}}$  in the same square.
\end{remark}
	
\subsection{Normal Distribution}
As an example of random vectors, we now consider specific family of distributions - the Multivariate Normal (also called Multivariate Gaussian) Distributions.
%The normal distribution is very useful for many reasons. One reason is the Central Limit Theorem (CLT). You have likely seen the one-dimensional ("univariate") version of the CLT in probability course. In one of its forms, under some conditions, the CLT states that sample mean of random i.i.d variables (when properly scaled) converges in distribution to the normal distribution. There is a multivariate version of the CLT, which is one reason why you want to be familiar with the multivariate normal distribution.
The (multivariate) normal distribution is useful for a number of reasons, an important one being that many results and methods (such as propagation of uncertainty and least squares parameter fitting) can be derived analytically in explicit form when the relevant variables are (multivariate) normally distributed.

\begin{definition}[Univariate Normal Distribution] \label{def: UVN}
	A random variable x has a (one dimensional, $d=1$, or "univariate") 	\textbf{\textit{normal distribution}} with expectation $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$ if it has a PDF of the form:
	$$f(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2} } e^{ -\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2} } $$
	In this case we write: $x\sim\mathcal{N}({\mu},{\sigma^2})$
	
\end{definition}
\begin{definition}[Multivariate Normal Distribution] \label{def: MVN}
	a random vector $\mathbf{x}$ has a 	\textbf{\textit{multivariate normal distribution}} with expectation $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ and covariance matrix (see definition below) $C$ if it has a joint PDF of the form:
	
	$$f\left(\mathbf{x}\right)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\left(2\pi\right)^{n}\left|C\right|}}\exp\left\{ -\frac{\left(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}\right)^{T}\boldsymbol{C^{-1}}\left(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}\right)}{2}\right\} $$
	In this case we write: $\mathbf{x}\sim\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu},C)$
\end{definition}

Observe that definition \ref{def: MVN} is generalization of \ref{def: UVN}, i.e. when $d = 1$ both definitions are same.

In some cases we do not care about some of the variables in the distribution. For example, suppose we have joint probability function of the variables "height" and "weight" but we're only interested in one of them. This lead as to the following definition:

\begin{definition}[Marginal Distribution]
	\textbf{\textit{Marginal distribution}} of a subset of a collection of random variables is the probability distribution of the variables contained in the subset, no matter what the values of the other variables. It can be written as:
	$$f(\mathbf{x})=\int_{\mathbf{y}}f(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})\mathrm{d}\mathbf{y}$$
\end{definition}
where the $\mathbf{y}$ integration is over all the uninteresting RV's. Note that again we are writing for short $f(\mathbf{x})$ and $f(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})$  instead of  $f_{\mathbf{X}}(\mathbf{x})$ and $f_{\mathbf{X},\mathbf{Y}}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})$.

\begin{exercise}[Marginal of the 1st coordinate of a 2D normal RV with diagonal covariance matrix]
	Let $\mathbf{x}\sim\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu},C)$ where $\mathbf{x}=(x_1,x_2)$,  $\boldsymbol{\mu} = (\mu_1, \mu_2), C=\left(\begin{array}{cc}
		\sigma_{1}^{2} & 0\\
		0 & \sigma_{2}^{2}
	\end{array}\right)$.
	
	Find the PDF of the marginal distribution of $x_1$.
\end{exercise}
\noindent
\textbf{Solution:}  Observe that we have
\begin{align*}
	& \frac{1}{\sqrt{\left(2\pi\right)^{n}\left|C\right|}}\exp\left\{ -\frac{\left(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}\right)^{T}C^{-1}\left(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}\right)}{2}\right\} \\ &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{\left(2\pi\right)^{2}\left|\left(\begin{array}{cc}
				\sigma_{1}^{2} & 0\\
				0 & \sigma_{2}^{2}
			\end{array}\right)\right|}}\exp\left\{ -\frac{\left(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}\right)^{T}\left(\begin{array}{cc}
			\sigma_{1}^{-2} & 0\\
			0 & \sigma_{2}^{-2}
		\end{array}\right)\left(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}\right)}{2}\right\}\\&
	= \frac{1}{\sqrt{\left(2\pi\right)^{2}\sigma_{1}^{2}\sigma_{2}^{2}}}\exp\left\{ -\frac{\left(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}\right)^{T}\left(\begin{array}{cc}
			\sigma_{1}^{-2} & 0\\
			0 & \sigma_{2}^{-2}
		\end{array}\right)\left(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}\right)}{2}\right\} \\&
	= \frac{1}{\sqrt{\left(2\pi\right)^{2}\sigma_{1}^{2}\sigma_{2}^{2}}}\exp\left\{ -\frac{1}{2} \left(\frac{x_{1}-\mu_{1}}{\sigma_{1}}\right)^{2} - \frac{1}{2} \left(\frac{x_{2} - \mu_{2}}{\sigma_{2}}\right)^{2}\right\} \\&
	=	\frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi\sigma_{1}^{2}}}\exp\left\{ -\frac{1}{2} \left(\frac{x_{1}-\mu_{1}}{\sigma_{1}}\right)^{2}\right\} \frac{1}{\sqrt{\left(2\pi\right)\sigma_{2}^{2}}}\exp\left\{ -\frac{1}{2} \left(\frac{x_{2}-\mu_{2}}{\sigma_{2}}\right)^{2}\right\}
\end{align*}

Now, since: $\int_{-\infty}^\infty\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma_{2}^{2}}}\exp\left\{ -\frac{1}{2} \left(\frac{x_{2}-\mu_{2}}{\sigma_{2}}\right)^{2}\right\}
\mathrm{d}x_2=1$, we get:

\begin{align*}
	&\int_{-\infty}^\infty\frac{1}{\sqrt{\left(2\pi\right)^{n}\left|C\right|}}\exp\left\{ -\frac{\left(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}\right)^{T}C^{-1}\left(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}\right)}{2}\right\}\;dx_2 \\
	&=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma_{1}^{2}}}\exp\left\{ -\frac{1}{2} \left(\frac{x_{1}-\mu_{1}}{\sigma_{1}}\right)^{2}\right\}
\end{align*}

and by definition \ref{def: UVN} we get:
$x_1\sim\mathcal{N}({\mu_1},{\sigma_1^2})$

\subsection{Covariance Matrix}

Recall the population variance $V(X)$ of a scalar (one dimensional) random variable $X$. What is the proper generalization for the case of a random vector?
\begin{definition}[Covariance Matrix]
	Consider a $d$-dimensional random vector $ \mathbf{X} =(X_{1},X_{2},...,X_d)^T$.
	We define the  {\em Covariance Matrix} $C$ as the $d \times d$ matrix whose $ (i,j)$ entry is the covariance $C_{ij}=\sigma (X_i,X_j)$
	\begin{itemize}
		\item In other words:
		\begingroup
		\footnotesize % \small in 11pt base font is 10pt
		$$C=\left(\begin{array}{ccc}
			\mathbb{E}\left[\left(X_{1}-\mathbb{E}\left[X_{1}\right]\right)\left(X_{1}-\mathbb{E}\left[X_{1}\right]\right)\right] & ... & \mathbb{E}\left[\left(X_{1}-\mathbb{E}\left[X_{1}\right]\right)\left(X_{d}-\mathbb{E}\left[X_{d}\right]\right)\right]\\
			\vdots & \ddots & \vdots\\
			\mathbb{E}\left[\left(X_{d}-\mathbb{E}\left[X_{d}\right]\right)\left(X_{1}-\mathbb{E}\left[X_{1}\right]\right)\right] & ... & \mathbb{E}\left[\left(X_{d}-\mathbb{E}\left[X_{d}\right]\right)\left(X_{d}-\mathbb{E}\left[X_{d}\right]\right)\right]
		\end{array}\right)$$
		\endgroup
		
		\item Recall that we can use  matrix notations for random vectors. In matrix notation,
		$$C=\mathbb{E}[({X} - {\mathbb{E}\left[X\right]} )({X} -{\mathbb{E}\left[X\right]} )^{T}]$$
		
		\item The diagonal elements of $C$ are $\sigma (X_i, X_i) \equiv \sigma^2 _{X_i} \equiv  V(X_i)$. $C$ is symmetric positive semidefinite.
		
	\end{itemize}
	
\end{definition}

\subsection{Estimating the Covariance Matrix}

Back to statistics, let us consider how to estimate the covariance matrix based on random sampling. In this context, $C$ is called the {\em population covariance matrix}.
Let's start with the case $d=2$. Consider a two-dimensional random vector $\mathbf{X}=(X_1,X_2)^T$, where, for example, $X_1$ is the height of a person and $X_2$ is the weight.

Taking a sample of $m$ people from the population, we equip the data elements with two indices, the first stands for the feature (1 for height, 2 for weight) and the second for the person's designated number. 
So the height samples are denoted by  $x_{1,1},..,x_{1,m}$ and the corresponding weight samples are $x_{2,1},..,x_{2,m}$. Note that we can write the data as a  matrix $X\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times m}$ where $m$ is the {\em sample size} and $d$ is the dimension (number of random variables). In our case, $d=2$ and
$$X = 
\left(\begin{array}{ccc}
	x_{1,1} & ... & x_{1,m}\\
		x_{2,1} & ... & x_{2,m}
\end{array}\right)=
\left(\begin{array}{ccc}
	\mathbf{x}_1 & ... &\mathbf{x}_m
\end{array}\right)
$$
where we denoted the column vectors of the data matrix $X$ by $\mathbf{x}_i=(x_{1,k}, x_{2,k})^T$, $k=1..m$. The boldface notation is used to remind us that the $\mathbf{x}_i$'s are vectors, not  scalars.

\begin{definition}[Sample Covariance]
Generalizing the estimators we chose for a one-dimensional RV we now define the \textbf{\textit{sample covariance}} by
$$\hat{\sigma}(X_i, X_j) = \frac{1}{m-1} \sum^{m}_{k=1}{(x_{i,k}-\hat{\mu}_i)(x_{j,k}-\hat{\mu}_j)}$$
where $\hat{\mu}_i$ is the sample mean of the random variable $X_i$.
\end{definition}
Here, $d=2$ so i,j=1,2, but the above definition holds for general $d$.
 
In particular, the  variance estimators, that is, the sample variances of the heights and weights are:
$$ \hat{\sigma}\left(X_i,X_i\right)  = \frac{1}{m-1} \sum^{m}_{k=1}(x_{i,k} - \hat{\mu}_i)^2, i=1,2 \\$$
where $\hat{\sigma}\left(X_i,X_i\right)\equiv \hat{\sigma}^2_{X_i} \equiv  \hat{\sigma}^2_i \equiv \hat{\sigma}_{i,i}$ are all equivalent notations.
We can now define the sample covariance matrix $\hat{C}$. Again, as customary in statistics, we use the "hat notation" which reminds that $\hat{C}$ is our prescription for estimating the population covariance matrix $C$, based on the data we have.
\vspace{3mm}

\begin{definition}[Sample Covariance Matrix]
Let $\textbf{X}=(X_1,\ldots,X_d)^T$ be a $d$-dimensional random vector. Let $\mathbf{x}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{x}_m$ be $m$ i.i.d samples of $\mathbf{X}$. Here, each sample $\mathbf{x}_i$ (note again the boldface notation) is a column vector in $\R^d$ corresponding to the columns of the data matrix $X$. The sample covariance matrix of $\mathbf{x}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{x}_m$ is a square $d$-by-$d$ matrix $\hat{C}$ with  $\hat{C}_{i,j}=\hat{\sigma}\left(X_{i},X_{j}\right)$ , $i,j=1..d$. 
\end{definition}

The sample covariance matrix is symmetric since $\hat{\sigma}\left(X_{j},X_{i}\right)=\hat{\sigma}\left(X_{i},X_{j}\right)$. The diagonal entries of the sample covariance matrix are the sample variances and the other entries are the sample covariances.
In matrix notation, the sample covariance matrix is given by \textcolor{blue}{Check}
$$\hat{C} = \frac{1}{m-1} \sum^{m}_{i=1}(\mathbf{x}_i-\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}})(\mathbf{x}^T_i-\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}}^T)$$


where $\boldsymbol{\hat{\mu}} \in\mathbb{R}^{d\times 1}$ is a column vector of the sample means (in the $d=2$ case, $\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}}^T = (\hat{\mu}_1, \hat{\mu}_2)$).
% Depending on the context, $\bar{X}$ may also denote the means matrix: $\bar{X} \in\mathbb{R}^{d\times m}$ with elements $\bar{X}_{i,j}=\hat{\mu}_i$.
If $\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}}=0$, that is, if the data is shifted to have zero mean, the covariance matrix is easily computed from the symmetric matrix $XX^T$, using the relation $\hat{C} = \frac{XX^T}{m-1}$.

\begin{exercise}
	Let $X^T=\left(\begin{array}{cc}
		150 & 45\\
		170 & 74\\
		184 & 79
	\end{array}\right)$ be samples of height and weight of 3 different people where $X_{1, i}$ is the height and $X_{2, i}$ is the weight of the i'th person. Calculate the covariance matrix of the sample.

\textbf{Solution:} First of all we will make the data centered by subtracting the corresponding sample mean from each entry of $X^T$.
%matrix $(1,1,1)^T\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}}^T$:
We have: $\hat{\mu}_1=168, \hat{\mu}_2=66$, so
$$X_{centered}^{T}=X^T  - \left(\begin{array}{cc}
	168 & 66\\
	168 & 66\\
	168 & 66
\end{array}
\right)
=\left(\begin{array}{cc}
	150 & 45\\
	170 & 74\\
	184 & 79
\end{array}\right) - \left(\begin{array}{cc}
	168 & 66\\
	168 & 66\\
	168 & 66
\end{array}
\right)
=\left(\begin{array}{cc}
	-18 & -21\\
	2 & 8\\
	16 & 13
\end{array}\right)$$
Now,
$$
\hat{C} = \frac{X_{centered}X_{centered}^{T}}{3-1}=\left(\begin{array}{cc}
	292 & 301\\
	301 & 337
\end{array}\right)
$$

\end{exercise}


\subsection{Linear Transformations of the Data Set}
Let us look at how linear transformations affect the data set and as a result, the covariance matrix. 
Although we will now focus on the two-dimensional case, the results in this section can be easily generalized to higher dimensional data. 
The covariance matrix for two dimensions, $d=2$, is

$$C = \left( \begin{array}{ccc}  \sigma(X_1, X_1) & \sigma(X_1, X_2) \\  \sigma(X_2, X_1) & \sigma(X_2, X_2) \end{array} \right)$$

As an initial data, we take a set of points generated by sampling our two independent random variables, $X_1$ and $X_2$  with two different distributions but with equal mean $\mu_1 = \mu_2=0$ and equal variances $\sigma^2_{X_1}=\sigma^2_{X_2}\equiv  \sigma^2$. 

\begin{figure}[h!]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[scale=0.3]{chapters/mathematical.basis/figures/uncorrelated.png}
	\caption{Initially Uncorrelated Variables \textcolor{blue}{TO DO Add $x_1$ and $x_2$ labels for axis}}
\end{figure}

In particular this would mean that $X_1$ and $X_2$ are uncorrelated and the covariance matrix $C$ is therefore diagonal. Similarly, the coordinates of the generated points $(x_{1,i},x_{2,i})$, shown in the figure, will be approximately uncorrelated. For the sake of simplicity, let us assume that we have generated enough points so that the sample covariance matrix elements are (almost) equal to the population covariance matrix,  $\hat{C}\approx C$, and therefore we have:
\vspace{3mm} 
% \footnote{Uncorrelated does not imply independent. See, e.g., \url{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normally_distributed_and_uncorrelated_does_not_imply_independent}}
$$\hat{C} = \left( \begin{array}{ccc}  \sigma^2 & 0 \\  0 & \sigma^2 \end{array} \right)$$







Next, we will look at how transformations affect our data and the covariance matrix $\hat{C}$. We start transforming our data by multiplying the data matrix with the following scaling matrix:

$$S = \left( \begin{array}{ccc}  s_{1} & 0 \\  0 & s_{2} \end{array} \right)$$

This transformation simply stretches (or shrinks) the $x_1$ and $x_2$ components by multiplying them by $s_{1}$ and $s_{2}$ respectively. Now the covariance matrix of our transformed data set will be:
$$\frac{SX(SX)^T}{m-1}=S\frac{XX^T}{m-1}S^T=SCS^T=\left( \begin{array}{ccc}  (s_{1}\sigma)^2 & 0 \\  0 & (s_{2}\sigma)^2 \end{array} \right)$$.

\begin{figure}[h!]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[scale=0.3]{chapters/mathematical.basis/figures/uncorrelated_scaled.png}
	\caption{Uncorrelated Scaled Variables}
\end{figure}

Now we will apply a linear transformation composed of the previous scaling matrix $S$ followed by a two dimensional rotation matrix $R$, which is given by:
$$R = \left( \begin{array}{ccc}  \cos\theta  & -\sin\theta  \\  \sin\theta  & \cos\theta  \end{array} \right)$$

where $\theta$ is the rotation angle. The transformed data is then given by $RSX$ and the covariance matrix is


$$\frac{RSX(RSX)^T}{m-1}=RS\frac{XX^T}{m-1}S^TR^T=RSCS^TR^T=R\left( \begin{array}{ccc}  (s_{1}\sigma)^2 & 0 \\  0 & (s_{2}\sigma)^2 \end{array} \right)R^T$$
$$=\sigma^2 \times \left( \begin{array}{ccc}  s^2_{1}\cos^2\theta +s^2_{2}\sin^2\theta  & \sin\theta \cos\theta (s^2_{1}- s^2_{2})\\  \sin\theta \cos\theta (s^2_{1}- s^2_{2}) & s^2_{1}\sin^2\theta +s^2_{2}\cos^2\theta  \end{array} \right)$$

Note that without applying an \emph{asymmetric} scaling, rotation alone would not be enough to create correlations since for $s_{1}= s_{2}$, the off-diagonal elements vanish.
\begin{figure}[h!]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[scale=0.3]{chapters/mathematical.basis/figures/correlated.png}
	\caption{Correlated Scaled Variables}
\end{figure}

As we can see now, the $x,y$ coordinate of each variable are not uncorrelated anymore. For example, if we know that the $x$ value of a point in the sample is high, it is more likely that the $y$ value is low, and vice versa. 

Note the form that we got on the right hand side of the first line of the above equation: an orthogonal matrix, $R$, multiplying a diagonal matrix, multiplied by another orthogonal matrix, $R^T$. This is the \textit{eigenvalue decomposition} (EVD) of the (transformed) covariance matrix. Later on, we are going to use some of the properties of the covariance matrix and its eigenvalue decomposition in the context of a technique called \textit{dimensionality reduction}.


\section{Probability Inequalities}
\subsection{Background}
Probability inequalities are estimations of  probabilities which are less accurate than exact values  but generally much easier to calculate since they are given in terms of simple properties of a distribution such as the mean and the variance. Such inequalities are used in many machine learning tasks, to obtain bounds on errors of the learning algorithm.
\vspace{3mm}

For example, the law of large numbers states that if we take the empirical average (the sample mean) of enough i.i.d. random variables, it will be, most likely, very close to the expected value.
Probability inequalities provide us a way to estimate how many samples are "enough".
\vspace{3mm}

Suppose that we have a bag containing red and blue balls and we would like to estimate the fraction $p$ of red balls in the bag by randomly picking one ball at a time and then putting it back in. The straightforward strategy is to draw $m$ samples and then to use the following estimation  ("prediction")
\[
\hat{p} =  \frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^{m}x_i =\frac{ ~\text{Number of red balls}}{m}~,
\]
where $x_i$ equals 1 if the $i$'th ball came out red and 0 otherwise. 
It is clear that $\E [\hat{p}] = p$. However, we might err due to the fact that we get only limited information. We are only sampling $m$ times so we might get unlucky and end up with a non-representative sample. Although unlikely, this can happen even if $m$ is very large, even larger than the total number of balls, because each time we returned the ball to the bag.
Thus, typically, we must settle for a certain accuracy - an additive error - that is, we will be happy to know that if we sample more than $m$ times, our estimation,  $\hat{p}$, is going
to be within a distance $\epsilon>0$ from the real $p$. However, no matter how large $m$ is, there is no absolute guarantee that  $\hat{p}$ will have that required accuracy.
Since $m$ is finite, there is always a finite chance we sample the same color again and again.  So, at most we can require to have enough confidence, say, with probability of $1-\delta$ where $\delta>0$ is small, that $\hat{p}$ is accurate enough ("accurate enough" defined as within  $\epsilon$ from our target, $p$). And now that we posed a realistic requirement, we can ask how large $m$ should be in order  to satisfy it.


The above example demonstrate the general type of questions we will use probability inequalities for: Given an accuracy parameter $\epsilon > 0$ and a confidence
parameter $\delta \in (0,1)$, how many samples are needed to guarantee that with probability of at least $1- \delta$, our estimate is within an additive error of at most $\epsilon$.
That is, we aim at constructing an algorithm (a "learner") whose estimations are probably (with probability at least $1-\delta$) approximately (within additive error of at most $\epsilon$) correct.
We then say that given $m$ samples or more,  our learner's estimation will be  \emph{probably approximately correct (PAC)}, a fundamental concept in machine learning.

The exact formulation of the PAC framework will be given in the following chapters. Here, we will only consider a preliminary example, that of estimating the bias of a given coin, that will reveal many of the ingredients of the PAC framework.

\subsection{Markov's and Chebyshev's inequalities} \label{sec:recap}
We now recall Markov's and Chebyshev's inequalities and then apply both inequalities to derive concentration inequalities for averages.
\begin{theorem} \textbf{Markov's inequality:} Let $X$ be a nonnegative random
	variable (i.e. $\text{Im}(X) \subseteq \R _{\ge 0}$)  and deonte the expectation value of $X$ by $\mathbb{E}[X]$. For any $t>0$,
	\[
	\mathbb{P} [X \geq t] \leq \frac{\mathbb{E}[X]}{t}~.
	\]
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
	Let $f(x)$ be the density function of $x$. Since $X$ is non-negative so $f(x)=0$ for $x<0$. Thus,
	\begin{align*}
		\frac{\mathbb{E}[X]}{t} &= \frac{1}{t}\int_{0 } ^\infty f(x) x \,dx \ge \frac{1}{t}\int_{x =t} ^\infty f(x) x \,dx \ge \frac{1}{t}\int_{x =t} ^\infty f(x) t \, dx=\mathbb{P}[X \ge t] ~.
	\end{align*}
\end{proof}
\begin{corollary} \label{cor:markovAve}
 Let $X_1,\ldots,X_m$ be $m$ i.i.d. non-negative  random variables and denote the expectation value of each by  $\mathbb{E}[X_i]=\mathbb{E}[X]$, $i=1..m$. 
 Denote also  $\bar{X}=\frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1} ^m X_i$. Then for any  $t>0$,
	\[
	\mathbb{P} [\bar{X} \geq t] \leq \frac{\mathbb{E}[X]}{t}~.
	\]
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
	We can apply Markov's inequality to the random variable $\bar{X}$ since it is non-negative  (being a sum of none-negative random variables). Thus, 
		\[
	\mathbb{P} [\bar{X} \geq t] \leq \frac{\mathbb{E}[\bar{X}]}{t}~.
	\]
	Now, 
	\begin{align*}
		\ensuremath{\mathbb{E}} [\bar{X}] = \ensuremath{\mathbb{E}} \left[\frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1} ^m X_i \right] 
		= \frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1} ^m \ensuremath{\mathbb{E}}[X_i] 
		= \frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1} ^m \ensuremath{\mathbb{E}}[X] 
		= \ensuremath{\mathbb{E}}[X]~.
	\end{align*}
 and therefore, we obtain the desired bound.
\end{proof}

Markov's inequality gives a bound in terms of the expectation value $\E(X)$, but it can be used to obtain also a bound in terms of the variance of $X$,  $V(X)= \E (X-\E[X])^2$. This bound is called Chebyshev's inequality and does not require $X$ to be non-negative.  
\begin{theorem} \textbf{Chebyshev's inequality:} For a random variable
	$X$ with finite mean  $\E[X]$ and a variance $V(X)$ and for every $t  > 0$,
	\[
	\mathbb{P}[|X-\E [X]| \geq t] \leq \frac{V[X]}{t^2}.
	\]
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
	The random variable $(X-\E[X])^2$ is non-negative (even if $X$ isn't). Applying Markov's inequality to it, we obtain 
	\[
	\mathbb{P}[(X-\E [X])^2 \geq t^2] \leq \frac{V[X]}{t^2},
	\]
To finish the proof, simply observe that $\mathbb{P}[|X-\E [X]| \geq t] = \mathbb{P}[(X-\E [X])^2 \geq t^2]$.
\end{proof}
Unlike the expectation, the variance of the average of i.i.d. random variables is not equal to the variance of the original random variable. Concretely, for a random variable $X$ and $m$ i.i.d. copies of $X$, denoted $X_1,\ldots, X_m$, we have
\begin{align} \label{eq:varAve}
	V \left[\frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1} ^m X_i \right] &= \frac{1}{m^2} V \left[\sum_{i=1} ^m X_i \right] \notag \\&
	= \frac{1}{m^2} \sum_{i=1} ^m V [X_i] \notag \\&
	= \frac{1}{m^2} \sum_{i=1} ^m V [X] \notag \\&
	= \frac{1}{m} V[X]~.
\end{align}
In particular, the variance of the average tends to zero when $m$ tends to infinity. This leads to a much better concentration inequality.
\begin{corollary} \label{cor:chebysevAve}
	Let $X$ be a nonnegative random variable with finite variance and let $X_1,\ldots,X_m$ be $m$ i.i.d. copies of $X$. Denote their average by $\bar{X}=\frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1} ^m X_i$. Finally, let $t>0$. Then
	\[
	\mathbb{P} [|\bar{X}-\E [\bar{X}]| \geq t] = \mathbb{P} [|\bar{X}-\E [X]| \geq t] \leq \frac{V[X]}{mt^2}~.
	\]
\end{corollary}

For a positive integer $k$, the $k$-th \emph{moment} of a random variable $X$ is defined by $\ensuremath{\mathbb{E}}[X^k]$. As we just saw, Markov's inequality exploits only information about the first moment, while Chebyshev's inequality  exploits both the first and the second moments. Comparing the bounds in \ref{cor:markovAve} and \ref{cor:chebysevAve}, we see that using both the first and the second moments, we obtain better concentration inequalities than using only the first. We shall see soon that more generally, the more moments we use, the better the bounds we get.

\subsection{Coin Prediction}  \label{sec:basicConcentration}
Let us formulate the problem of estimating the bias of a
coin (in short, coin prediction)\footnote{We take this opportunity to recall some basic notions from probability theory}. Formally, a coin is a Bernoulli random variable $Z$ with a bias $p - 1/2$ ('bias' is defined here as the \textit{deviation} from $p=1/2$, where $p$ is the probability to obtain ``heads'').
%The probability space which is associated with $Z$ is the triplet $(\Omega ,\mathbb{P}  )$, where the sample space $\Omega$ equals $\{H,T\}$,  $$\prod\{1\}]=p~,~\Dc [\{0\}]=1-p~. $$Once we defined $\Dc (\{1\})$ and $\Dc (\{0\})$, the values of $\Dc $ on other events are determined by combining the fact that $\Dc $ is additive (for disjoints subsets $E,F \in \cF$, we have $\Dc (E \cup F)=\Dc (E) + \Dc (F)$) with the fact that $\Dc (\Omega)=1$.
The random variable $Z$ is simply a function from $\Omega$ to $\{0,1\}$. Let $Z_1,
\ldots, Z_m$ be a sequence of independent and identically distributed
(i.i.d.) random variables according to the distribution $\Dc _Z$ (that
is, each of the $Z_i$ has the same distribution as $Z$, and all of the
$Z_i$ are mutually independent). Denote the random sequence  (a.k.a. sample)
$(Z_1,\ldots, Z_m)$ by $S$, where $|S|=m$. The distribution which is associated with $S$ is denoted by $\Dc _Z ^m$ (or simply by $\Dc ^m$).

The input of a \emph{learning algorithm} $A$ for the task of coin prediction consists of a random sequence $S$ which is drawn according to
$\Dc ^m$. Based on this input, the algorithm has to predict the value of $p$. We denote this prediction, (that is, the output of the algorithm) by $A(S)$ or simply by $\hat{p}$. Clearly, the drawn sequence can not fully describe the distribution. Hence, we introduce an \emph{accuracy} parameter $\epsilon>0$, and allow $\hat{p}$ to deviate from $p$ by at most $\epsilon$. Furthermore, there is always a chance that the drawn sequence would be highly non-representative, so it is impossible to obtain a guarantee that holds with absolute certainty. Hence, we introduce a \emph{confidence} parameter $\delta>0$, and allow that the event $B:=\{s : |\hat{p}-p|>\epsilon\}$ occur with probability at
most $\delta$. More formally:

\begin{definition}  \label{def:sampleCoin}
	A mistake-bound learning algorithm $A$ for the task of (batch) coin prediction is a function $\hat{p}(S)$
	where $S\in \bigcup_{m=1} ^\infty \{0,1\}^m$ and $\hat{p}(S)\in [0,1]$ which satisfies the following condition:
	
	For any $0\le \epsilon,\delta\le 1$, there exists a non-negative integer $m_A(\epsilon,\delta)$ such that,
	if a sequence $S$ of $m$ numbers, where $m\ge m_A$, is generated according to $\Dc ^m_p$, (that is, $\Dc ^m$ with parameter $p$, which is added temporarily here as a subscript but omitted later on) then the probability that the algorithm's output $\hat{p}(S)$ will not be in $[p-\epsilon,p+\epsilon]$ is bounded by:
	$$ \Dc ^{m}_p \left[ |\hat{p}-p| > \epsilon\right] <  \delta,$$
	\textbf{for any $0\le p\le 1$,} and if a sequence $S$ of $m$ numbers, where $m < m_A$, is generated, \textbf{there exist a $p$}, with $0\le p\le 1$ such that:
	$$ \Dc ^{m}_p \left[ |\hat{p}-p| >  \epsilon \right] > \delta$$
	
	The function $m_A(\epsilon,\delta)$: $\left[0,1\right]\times \left[0,1\right]\rightarrow \mathbb{N}$ is called the \emph{sample complexity} of the learning algorithm $A$.
	
	% More generally: the sample complexity of a machine learning algorithm represents the number of training-samples that it needs in order to successfully learn a target function.
\end{definition}

The first condition means that, no matter what $p$ is, it is enough to draw $m_A$ samples (i.e., to flip the coin $m_A$ times) in order to know $p$, with a certainty of $1-\delta$ and an accuracy of $\pm \epsilon$. The second conditions means that, at least for some values of $p$, drawing $m_A(\epsilon,\delta)-1$ samples would \textit{not} be enough for that.

\textbf{Note about the confidence requirement}: In the above definition the confidence requirement must hold \emph{independently of the procedure by which the coins are provided}, that is, independently of the way $p$ is chosen. It does not matter if, for example, the coin is randomly drawn from a pile of coins where most coins are approximately fair (most of their $p$'s are close to $1/2$) or where most are faked in a specific way, (their $p$'s are concentrated around, say, $\frac{1}{4}$ ),  or where all $p$'s are equally probable.

Another way to think of the sample complexity is the following. Consider all possible sequences, $S$, of $m$ 0's and 1's. For each value of $p$, each of these sequences has a different probability to occur. Each $S$ can be labeled as 'good' ('typical') or 'bad'('atypical'), 'good' means that $m \cdot \hat{p}(S)$ is between $m(p-\epsilon)$ and $m(p+\epsilon)$ and 'bad' otherwise (Note that,  even for the same coin,  if  $S$ is good with respect to algorithm $A_1(S)$ it may still be bad with respect to another algorithm $A_2(S),$  if $\hat{p}_1(S)\ne \hat{p}_2(S)$.) Given a coin, let us denote the smallest $m$ for which the  total sum of the probabilities of all the good sequences is at least $1-\delta$, by $m_A^{(p)}(\epsilon,\delta)$. We can think of $m_A^{(p)}$ as the \emph{coin-specific} sample complexity. The algorithm sample complexity $m_A(\epsilon,\delta)$ is the maximal number among the coin-specific sample complexities, that is: $m_A(\epsilon,\delta) = \max_{p}(m_A^{(p)}(\epsilon,\delta))$.

%The term `approximately' in PAC stems from the fact that we only require that $\hat{p}$ approximates $p$ to an accuracy $\epsilon$. The term `probably' in PAC comes from the fact that we are satisfied whenever $\hat{p}$ satisfies the accuracy requirements with probability at least$1-\delta$.

Given an i.i.d. random sample $S=(z_1, \ldots, z_m)$, a reasonable
estimate of $p$ is the empirical proportion of heads (ones), namely
$\hat{p}=\frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1} ^m z_m$. The first basic property of
this average is that its expected value equals $p$. This follows by
the linearity of expectation as follows:
\[
\ensuremath{\mathbb{E}} \left [\frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1} ^m Z_i \right] = \frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1} ^m
\ensuremath{\mathbb{E}}[Z_i] = \frac{1}{m} \cdot m \cdot p = p~.
\]
We say in this case that $\hat{p}$ forms an \emph{unbiased estimate}
of $p$. What can we say about the quality of $\hat{p}$ as an estimate
of $p$? In other words, how tightly $\hat{p}$ is concentrated around
its expectation? To answer this question, we can use concentration inequalities.\\

\noindent \textbf{First attempt: Markov's inequality: }

For a direct application of Markov's inequality we take  $|\hat{p}-p|$ as our RV  (note that $\hat{p}-p$ is not a positive RV and therefore cannot be used in Markov's inequality). We then need to calculate $\frac{1}{\epsilon}\ensuremath{\mathbb{E}}[|\hat{p}-p|]$ and extract its dependence on the sample size $m$.
Since we are going to get much better bounds below, here we only state the result (and leave the calculation as a challange exercise):
$$
\Dc ^m \left[|\hat{p}-p| \geq \epsilon \right]  \le \frac{1}{\sqrt{4m\epsilon^2}}
$$
This implies that the sample complexity of coin prediction is bounded above by
$m(\epsilon,\delta) \le \left \lceil \frac{1}{4\epsilon^2} \cdot
\frac{1}{\delta^2} \right \rceil$.
(You can verify by substituting $m=\frac{1}{4\epsilon^2}\cdot
\frac{1}{\delta^2}$ in the bound)

\vspace{5mm}

\begin{exercise}[Markov's inequality]
	Prove that:  $\Dc ^m \left[|\hat{p}-p| \geq \epsilon \right]  \le \frac{1}{\sqrt{4m\epsilon^2}}.$
\end{exercise}

\begin{itemize}
	\item Hint: Show that for any RV $h$ with $\ensuremath{\mathbb{E}}(h)=0$ :
	$$\ensuremath{\mathbb{E}}(|h|) \le \sqrt{ Var(h)}$$
	and then apply this result to the RHS of Markov's inequality.
	
	\item Use the above to obtain a bound over the sample complexity and compare it to the one obtained from the Chebyshev's inequality.
\end{itemize}
\color{black}

\noindent \textbf{Second attempt: Chebyshev's inequality: }
The variance of a Bernoulli random variable is $p(1-p) \le 1/4$. Applying \ref{cor:chebysevAve}, we obtain that
$$ \Dc ^m [|\hat{p}-p| \ge \epsilon] = \Dc ^m[|\hat{p}-\mathbb{E} [\hat{p}]| \ge \epsilon] \le \frac{Var[Z]}{m\epsilon^2} \le \frac{1}{4m \epsilon ^2}$$

The bound obtained using Chebyshev's inequality tends to zero as $\frac{1}{m}$ while the one obtained from Markov's inequality tends to zero as $\frac{1}{\sqrt{m}}$.
This fact enables us to obtain a better bound for the sample complexity:

\begin{corollary}  \label{cor:basicConcentration}
	The sample complexity of coin prediction is bounded above by
	$m(\epsilon,\delta) \le \left \lceil \frac{1}{4\epsilon^2} \cdot
	\frac{1}{\delta} \right \rceil$.
	(You can verify by substituting $m=\frac{1}{4\epsilon^2} \cdot
	\frac{1}{\delta}$ in the bound)
\end{corollary}


\color{black}


A natural question which arises is whether the obtained bound is optimal (tight). There is a good reason to suspect that this bound is not optimal; When we applied Markov's or Chebyshev's inequality, we did not exploit the fact that our RV's are bounded (between $0$ and $1$).  Indeed, in the next section we prove a much better bound \footnote{Which is tight, although we will not prove that}. For this end, we will introduce Hoeffding's inequality for the average of independent and bounded random variables. This inequality will give us exponential (instead of polynomial!) convergence in terms of the sample size $m$.

\subsubsection{Hoeffding's Inequality}
\begin{theorem}[Hoeffding's inequality]
	Let $X_1, \ldots X_m$ be independent and bounded random variables with $a_i \leq X_i \leq b_i$. Let $\bar{X} = \frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1} ^m X_i$. Then,
	\[
	\mathbb{P}  [ |\bar{X} - \E[\bar{X}]| \geq \epsilon ] \leq 2 \exp \left( \frac{-2m^2 \epsilon^2}{\sum _{i =1} ^m (b_i-a_i)^2} \right)
	\]
\end{theorem}
\begin{corollary}
	Let $X_1, \ldots X_m$ be a sequence of i.i.d. (independent \textbf{and} identically distributed) and bounded with $a \leq X_i \leq b$ (same $a, b$ for all $X_i$'s). Let $\bar{X} = \frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1} ^m X_i$ and let $\mu = \E[X_i]$. Then,
	\[
	\mathbb{P}  [|\bar{X} - \mu| \geq \epsilon] \leq 2 \exp \left( \frac{-2 m \epsilon^2}{(b-a)^2} \right)
	\]
\end{corollary}

\subsubsection{Coin Prediction Revisited}
Lets apply Hoeffding's inequality for the case of coin prediction which was discussed above. For a sample of size $m$, we obtain
\[
\Dc ^m [|\hat{p}-p| \ge \epsilon ]\leq  2 \exp(-2 m \epsilon ^2)~.
\]
Indeed, we achieve an exponential improvement (in terms of $\delta$).
\begin{corollary}  \label{cor:basicConcentration}
	The sample complexity of coin prediction is bounded above by
	$m(\epsilon,\delta) \le \left \lceil \frac{1}{2\epsilon^2} \cdot
	\log(\frac{2}{\delta}) \right \rceil$.
	(You can verify by substituting $m=\frac{1}{2\epsilon^2} \cdot
	\log(\frac{2}{\delta})$ in the bound)
\end{corollary}
